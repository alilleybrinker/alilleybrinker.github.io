- over the last 10 years, open source software transitioned from a community
  populated largely by hobbyists to one with a lot of corporate players
- we (collectively) wanted companies to invest in open source, and we
  envisioned that as being companies donating money to open source projects'
  existing developers; instead the monkey's paw curled, and we began to see
  companies hire developers to work on OSS, and also saw companies open source
  software which remained under corporate governance
- some of this is laundered through open source foundations; some foundations
  are structured as 501(c)6's, not 501(c)3's (for those in the US). this is
  an important distinction!

The expectations and operations of open source are extremely different between
the two types.

Also, given the tyranny of structurelessness, corporations getting involved in
open source _which does not have protections in place against this_ can often
quickly gain and wield enormous power by contributing. This is not inherently
bad, but _can_ quietly skew the direction and values of the project.

Open source is participatory, and the things that get worked on are the things
that contributors "want" to work on, and those "wants" are different when
contributors are all full time paid contributors from for-profit companies
